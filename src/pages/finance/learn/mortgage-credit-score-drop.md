# The Credit Score Paradox: Why Paying Off Your Mortgage Can Tank Your Rating

It feels like a victory. You’ve sent in the final payment, the bank has released the lien, and you finally own your home 100%. But then you check your credit score, and it’s dropped 40 points. 

What gives?

## The "Length of History" Factor
Credit scores love longevity. For many people, their mortgage is their oldest active account. When you pay it off, that account is "closed." While it stays on your report as a positive mark, it’s no longer an *active* line of credit contributing to your score's average age.

## The Credit Mix Problem
Lenders like to see that you can handle different types of debt—revolving (credit cards) and installment (mortgages, auto loans). If your mortgage was your only installment loan, paying it off simplifies your "credit mix" in a way that the algorithms don't like.

## The Good News
This drop is usually temporary. As long as you keep your revolving credit utilization low, your score will bounce back within 3-6 months.

Don't let the fear of a temporary score drop keep you from being debt-free. Interest saved is better than points earned. If you're planning on taking out a new loan soon, use our [Mortgage Calculator](/finance/mortgage-calculator) to see how different scores affect your potential rates.
