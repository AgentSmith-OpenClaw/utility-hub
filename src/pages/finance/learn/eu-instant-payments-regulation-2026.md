# The End of the "3-Day Wait": EU Instant Payments Are Mandatory

Remember when sending money to a friend or paying a bill meant waiting "1-3 business days" for it to arrive? Or worse, realizing on Friday afternoon that your transfer wouldn't land until Monday morning?

As of **2026**, those delays are effectively illegal for most standard Euro transfers. The European Union's **Instant Payments Regulation** has moved from a "nice-to-have" feature to a mandatory standard for all Payment Service Providers (PSPs) offering Euro credit transfers.

Here is what the new market looks like for consumers and businesses across the Eurozone (and beyond).

## The Core Rule: 10 Seconds, 24/7/365

Under the new Single Euro Payments Area (SEPA) rules:

1.  **Speed:** Transfers must be processed and funds made available in the recipient's account within **10 seconds**.
2.  **Availability:** This applies 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, 365 days a year. No "banking hours." No "bank holidays."
3.  **Confirmation:** The payer (you) must receive confirmation of the successful transfer (or rejection) within 10 seconds.

This applies to **all** electronic credit transfers in Euro within the EU/EEA. Whether you are sending rent to your landlord in Berlin, paying a supplier in Dublin, or splitting a dinner bill in Paris, the money moves instantly.

## The Big Win: No Extra Fees

Historically, banks often charged a premium for "Instant SEPA" transfers—sometimes €1.00 to €5.00 per transaction—while standard (slow) transfers were free.

**That practice is now banned.**

The regulation explicitly states that charges for instant payments **cannot be higher** than charges for standard credit transfers. Since standard SEPA transfers are usually free for consumers, **instant payments must now also be free.**

This levels the playing field. Fintechs like Revolut and N26 forced the issue, and now traditional banks (Deutsche Bank, BNP Paribas, Santander, etc.) are legally required to catch up without gauging customers.

## IBAN Check: Stopping Scams Before They Start

One major risk with instant payments is **Authorized Push Payment (APP) fraud**—where you are tricked into sending money to a scammer. Once it's gone instantly, it's hard to get back.

To combat this, the regulation introduces a mandatory **"Verification of Payee"** service (similar to "Confirmation of Payee" in the UK).

**How it works:**
Before you authorize the payment, your bank must verify that the **IBAN** you entered matches the **Account Name** of the recipient.
*   **Match:** Green light. Proceed.
*   **No Match/Close Match:** The bank must warn you ("The name you entered does not match the account holder"). You can still proceed, but you do so at your own risk.

This simple check is expected to prevent millions of euros in fraud annually.

## What About Non-Euro Countries?

If you live in an EU country that does not use the Euro (e.g., Sweden, Poland, Hungary, Romania, Bulgaria, Czechia), the rules still apply, but with a slightly longer implementation timeline.

*   **By 2027:** Banks in non-Eurozone EU countries must also offer instant Euro payments (and instant payments in their national currency if applicable legislation follows).
*   **Cross-Border:** Sending Euros from a Polish Zloty account to a German Euro account? It must still be instant if the currency conversion is handled by the bank seamlessly, though FX fees may still apply.

## Impact on Businesses & Cash Flow

For businesses, this is a revolution.
*   **Cash Flow:** Suppliers get paid instantly. No more "the check is in the mail."
*   **E-commerce:** Merchants can ship goods immediately upon payment receipt, reducing delivery times.
*   **B2B:** Large invoice settlements happen in real-time, reducing the need for short-term credit lines.
